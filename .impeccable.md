# CourseFuzz design context

## Users

The primary user is a computer-science instructor or course staff member who owns an
autograder and needs to know whether a plausible wrong solution can slip through it.
They review under time pressure, need evidence they can defend to students, and will
not authorize a test change they cannot inspect exactly. The golden task is: find one
real weakness, understand the counterexample, approve the exact patch, and verify that
the written artifact catches the mistake without rejecting accepted solutions.

## Brand personality

Forensic, calm, decisive. CourseFuzz should feel like a rigorous lab partner rather
than an omniscient AI assistant: it proposes hypotheses, labels their source, lets
execution establish truth, and keeps receipts. The voice is direct and plainspoken.

## Aesthetic direction

A daylight "proof sheet" inspired by annotated assessment rubrics, print test reports,
and incident-review tools. Warm paper, near-black ink, cobalt structure, and vermilion
proof marks replace the usual dark AI dashboard. Archivo supplies compact, forceful
headings; Atkinson Hyperlegible Next carries high-density evidence and UI text. The
signature motif is a continuous numbered proof rail from scan to read-back, with every
important claim attached to an executable result or hash. No gradients, glass, card
grids, decorative dashboards, or hidden mobile state.

## Design principles

1. Lead with the decision. Show the surviving misconception and measurable score gap
   before implementation detail.
2. Evidence stays adjacent to claims. Counterexample inputs, outputs, killed mutants,
   accepted-solution pass rate, and payload hash belong in the same reading path.
3. Approval is a hard visual and technical boundary. Display the exact test source and
   hash before enabling the write.
4. Progress is causal, not theatrical. The proof rail exposes stage, dependencies, and
   real audit events; motion only clarifies a live transition.
5. Critical state survives every viewport. Mode, confidence/evidence, approval scope,
   run status, and verification remain visible on mobile.
6. Accessibility is part of credibility. Semantic HTML, visible focus, AA contrast,
   useful text at 200% zoom, reduced-motion behavior, and descriptive status text are
   release requirements.

## Research basis

- The [OpenAI Build Week rules](https://openai.devpost.com/rules) weight technological
  implementation, design, potential impact, and idea quality equally, and require the product
  to work consistently with its description. That makes the complete executable loop the
  visual centerpiece, not a speculative feature map.
- [Gradescope's instructor workflow](https://www.gradescope.com/help) centers uploading an
  autograder and rerunning submissions after an update. CourseFuzz therefore presents the
  output as an instructor-approved grading artifact, not an AI report.
- [GitHub Actions' visualization graph](https://docs.github.com/en/actions/how-tos/monitor-workflows/use-the-visualization-graph)
  combines real-time status, dependency order, and drill-down logs. The CourseFuzz proof rail
  borrows that causal model while keeping one decision dominant.
- [Sentry issue detail](https://docs.sentry.io/product/issues/issue-details/) pairs a concise
  high-level issue summary with the exact event, trace, and breadcrumbs below it. CourseFuzz
  similarly layers the mutation-score change above the counterexample and audit events.
- [Archivo](https://www.omnibus-type.com/fonts/archivo/) was designed for high-performance
  typography across print and digital surfaces; [Atkinson Hyperlegible Next](https://www.brailleinstitute.org/freefont/)
  provides seven weights and letterforms designed for legibility. Together they support the
  proof-sheet direction without relying on common startup-interface defaults.
